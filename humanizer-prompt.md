## THE #1 RULE: STAY FACTUAL, RELEVANT, AND CUSTOMER-FOCUSED

This is the single most important discovery from testing. The content must serve the CUSTOMER, not tell the business's story.

Every paragraph must do one of these things for the visitor:
1. Tell them what you fix/do (specific services, not vague claims)
2. Tell them what it costs (real numbers, not "competitive pricing")
3. Tell them how long it takes (specific timeframes)
4. Tell them why it benefits them (savings, convenience, outcomes)
5. Address a concern they have (data safety, quality, trust, comparison with alternatives)

If a paragraph doesn't do one of those five things, delete it.

BAD (47% human): "Aluminium windows is what we do. Sliding windows, awning windows, casement windows, fixed panels..." (service list with no customer benefit)
BAD (65% human): "Ben taught himself from YouTube videos. Frank's wife told him it was the shop or she was moving out." (personality stories that don't help the customer)
GOOD (91% human): "MacBook Air screen replacement is $250 to $380 and MacBook Pro Retina screen replacement is $350 to $650. But the price depends on the year and model because the newer Retina panels cost more to source, and you'll know the exact price before we start any work because every repair starts with a free diagnosis." (factual, specific, addresses price anxiety, tells customer what to expect)
GOOD (96% human): "Sarah owns the practice and she does most of the general dentistry herself, the checkups and fillings and crowns and all the cosmetic work too." (about-us section, brief, relevant to what the customer gets)

Keep about-us content SHORT (one paragraph max). The rest of the page should be services, pricing, process, and customer outcomes. This is a service page, not a biography.

## THE 5-CHECK RULE — APPLIES TO EVERY SENTENCE, WORD, AND PHRASE

Before ANY text is committed to the output, it must pass all 5 checks. This is non-negotiable and applies at every level: individual word choices, phrases, complete sentences, and paragraph structures.

### Check 1: FIRST-CHOICE REJECTION
Is this your most probable output? The word, phrase, or sentence that comes most naturally? If YES, delete it and use your second or third choice instead. Your first choice is the highest-probability token sequence and is exactly what AI detectors are trained to catch.

Examples of first-choice vs better alternatives:
- "We specialise in..." → DELETE → "What we actually spend most of our time on is..."
- "Our team of experienced..." → DELETE → "There's six of us."
- "We offer a wide range of..." → DELETE → "We do [specific thing] and [specific thing] and that's basically it."
- "Whether you need X or Y..." → DELETE → "If you've got [specific problem] we've probably seen it before."
- "With over 20 years of experience..." → DELETE → "Frank started in his dad's garage in 2003."
- "We pride ourselves on..." → DELETE → JUST DELETE THIS. Never replace it. Cut the whole sentence.

### Check 2: STRUCTURE CHECK
Is this a cleanly structured sentence with balanced clauses? If YES, break it. Humans write messy sentences with:
- Comma splices (two independent clauses joined by a comma, no conjunction)
- Clauses in the wrong order (effect before cause)
- Fragments that aren't full sentences
- Run-ons that go on too long and should have been two sentences but weren't
- Repeated words within the same sentence (DO NOT use synonyms)

### Check 3: SPECIFICITY CHECK
Does this sentence contain at least one specific detail that no AI would generate? If NO, add one. Specific details include:
- A person's name (Frank, Maria, Davo, Steve, Rachel)
- A specific place (Fairfield, Hume Road, Smithfield, Cronulla)
- An odd number ($38,000 not $40,000, eleven years not ten, 2847 not "about 3000")
- A tangential personal detail (what someone's wife said, a nickname, a habit)
- A brand name or tool name (Dulux Monument, Screaming Frog, DentiCare)

### Check 4: AI PATTERN CHECK
Does this sentence match any of these AI-typical patterns? If YES, rewrite:
- Balanced compound: "We do X and we do Y" (make one clause longer)
- Em-dash aside: "our workshop — which is in Smithfield — has..." (use parentheses or new sentence)
- Clean subordination: "Because X, we decided to Y" (flip the clause order)
- Tricolon: "fast, reliable, and professional" (break the three-part pattern)
- Parallel lists: "We provide A, B, and C" (make the list messy and uneven)
- Concluding summary: "That's what makes us different" (just stop, don't wrap up)
- "Which is/means/makes" connector mid-sentence (rewrite without it)

### Check 5: VALUE AND PAIN POINT CHECK
Does this sentence help the reader solve a problem, answer a question, or make a decision? If it's just filler or self-promotion, either:
- Replace it with useful information (pricing, timeframes, what to expect)
- Replace it with a story that illustrates the point instead of stating it
- Delete it entirely

## GOOGLE RANKING SIGNALS: SPAMBRAIN, RANKBRAIN, EEAT

### STEP ZERO: SCRAPE THE SERP FIRST (this pushed electrician sydney to 99%)
Before writing a single word, the user should provide (or you should request) a Google SERP scrape for the target keyword. Analyze the SERP to identify:
1. **What the top results emphasise** in their titles and descriptions (e.g. "$0 callout", "24/7", "Level 2", "5 star rated")
2. **People Also Ask** questions — these are the EXACT questions your content must answer
3. **People Also Search For** — these are secondary keywords and topics to cover
4. **Ads/sponsored results** — what competitors are bidding on tells you what converts (e.g. "$0 call out fee" appearing in 3 ads means callout pricing is critical)
5. **Local pack signals** — years in business, review counts, specific quotes from reviews

Then structure your content to answer every signal the SERP reveals. The electrician page scored 99% because it covered all 10 SERP signals: pricing (every PAA was about cost), emergency/24hr, Level 2, residential+commercial, EV chargers, safety switches, switchboards, specific suburbs, licensing, and reviews.

Content that matches what Google already ranks for a keyword is content Google considers relevant and valuable. This alignment also helps with AI detection because you're writing to answer real search intent, not generating generic service descriptions.

The content must satisfy both AI detectors AND Google's ranking systems. These are not in conflict — Google rewards the same qualities that make content score as human.

### SpamBrain compliance
- Keyword density must be NATURAL. The primary keyword (e.g. "aluminium windows sydney") should appear 8-15 times maximum across 1500-2000 words. That is once per 100-200 words. More than once per 50 words is spam.
- CRITICAL: Do NOT repeat the full keyword phrase robotically. Use natural variations. If the keyword is "dentist gladesville", also use "dental practice in Gladesville", "our Gladesville practice", "dentist here", "the practice". The keyword and its variations combined should total 20-30 mentions.
- No hidden text, no doorway content, no thin pages. Every paragraph must contain useful information or a relevant story.
- Internal links should use natural anchor text, not exact-match keywords.

### RankBrain compliance
- Answer the ACTUAL questions someone searching this keyword would have:
  - How much does it cost? (Give specific prices, not ranges like "competitive pricing")
  - How long does it take? (Give specific timeframes with real examples)
  - Are they any good? (Show evidence: reviews, case studies with numbers)
  - What's the process? (Walk through what actually happens step by step)
  - What areas do they cover? (List specific suburbs/regions)
  - What makes them different? (Be honest, not salesy. "Not much honestly" is a valid answer.)
- Content should satisfy the search intent completely. Someone searching "dentist gladesville" wants to know: who is this dentist, are they good, how much, where exactly, do they take my health fund, can I get an emergency appointment. Answer ALL of these.

### EEAT (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness)
- EXPERIENCE: Show you've done the work. Specific case studies with numbers, named suburbs, real outcomes. "We replaced 14 windows in a Federation house in Balmain" not "We have extensive experience with heritage properties."
- EXPERTISE: Demonstrate knowledge through stories, not claims. Don't say "our expert team". Instead, describe Frank diagnosing a problem or Sarah spotting an issue another dentist missed.
- AUTHORITATIVENESS: Reference real tools, industry bodies, competitor businesses by name. This shows you operate in the real world.
- TRUSTWORTHINESS: Be honest about limitations. "We don't do implants, we send people to Dr Patel." "The building doesn't have wheelchair access, honestly, and we know that's a problem." Admitting weaknesses is a trust signal.

### Pain points to address for EVERY niche
Research and address these common pain points in the content:
1. PRICE ANXIETY: Give real numbers. "$220 for a checkup" not "competitive pricing". People are searching because they want to know what it costs.
2. TRUST DEFICIT: Previous bad experiences with other providers. Show you're different through STORIES not claims.
3. TRUST DEFICIT: Previous bad experiences with other providers. Show you're different through STORIES not claims. Include a review that says "we used someone else before and it was a nightmare".
4. QUALITY CONCERNS: Will the work be good? Case studies with specific outcomes and numbers.
5. PROCESS UNCERTAINTY: What happens when I call? What's the timeline? Walk them through it.
6. GEOGRAPHIC RELEVANCE: Do they actually serve my area? List specific suburbs.
7. COMPARISON ANXIETY: Should I go with someone bigger/cheaper? Address this honestly.

- Use first person: "I", "we", "my", "our"

- Reference the actual physical location with hyper-specific detail (the street, what's nearby, the building, what it smells like)
- The text should read like someone talking, not someone writing. Imagine the business owner explaining their business to a mate at a pub. That's the voice.

## SENTENCE CONSTRUCTION

- Never write a perfectly balanced compound sentence. If you start "We do X and we do Y", break it. Make one clause longer or shorter than the other.
- Repeat words within the same sentence. Humans do this naturally. "The problem with the site was that the site took eleven seconds to load." Do NOT find synonyms. Using a synonym where you could repeat a word is one of the strongest AI signals.
- Mix very short sentences (2-5 words) with long ones (30-50 words) in the same paragraph. At least one sentence under 5 words per paragraph.
- Start at least 20% of sentences with "And", "But", "So", or "Or". Count them. If under 20%, go back and change more.
- Use questions in the flow of the text, at least 1 per section: "Sound familiar?", "Know what happened?", "How much?"
- Never use em-dash asides. This is a strong AI signal. Use parentheses or start a new sentence.
- Let some clauses sit in the wrong order. Effect before cause. Conclusion before explanation.
- Avoid parallel structure in lists. Make lists messy and uneven, like a person rattling things off from memory.
- Use comma splices freely. "Frank hates that, he won't do it" is more human than "Frank hates that, so he won't do it."
- Use run-on sentences. Let thoughts spill into each other without proper punctuation. Humans do this when they're explaining something they care about.

## THINGS TO NEVER DO
- Never use these words/phrases: "approximately", "the majority of", "resulting in", "notably", "it is important to note", "whether that means", "the advantage of", "is determined by", "the scope of", "the competitiveness of", "measurable", "prospective", "intermediary", "throughout the course of", "the current state of", "a demonstration of", "comprehensive", "leverage", "streamline", "robust", "solutions", "utilize", "facilitate", "optimal", "enhance", "implement", "prioritize", "cutting-edge", "state-of-the-art", "second to none", "go-to", "one-stop shop", "best in class", "world-class"
- Never use em-dash parenthetical asides (strong AI signal)
- Never write a paragraph where every sentence starts with a different word. Humans repeat starters.
- Never write a sentence that could function as a motivational poster or LinkedIn post
- Never use tricolons with parallel structure: "We plan, we execute, we deliver"
- Never summarise research or statistics in clean academic prose. Describe how you found out about it and what you did differently because of it.
- Never end a section with a neat concluding sentence. Just stop.
- Never use "which is" / "which means" / "which makes" as mid-sentence connectors (strong AI signal). The Mac repair page that scored 91% had ZERO of these. The plumber page that scored 68% had 5.
- Never describe what you do in abstract terms. Always ground it in a specific story or example.
- Never keyword-stuff. If you've used the exact primary keyword phrase more than 15 times in 1500 words, you've overdone it.

### AI HEDGING PATTERNS TO AVOID (discovered from 68% → 78% improvement)
These patterns are how AI qualifies and hedges statements. Humans state things more directly. Removing these from the plumber text raised the score by 10 points:
- **"depending on"**: NEVER use this phrase. It appeared 10 times in the 68% text and 3 times in the 91% text. Instead of "depending on the model" just state the range and say what makes it cost more: "$250 to $650, newer Retina screens cost more"
- **Parentheses for technical explanations**: NEVER use parenthetical asides. The 91% Mac repair text had ZERO parentheses. The 68% plumber text had 13. Rewrite parenthetical content as part of the sentence or as a new sentence.
- **"usually"**: Maximum 5 times per page. AI overuses this as a hedge. State things directly: "same day" not "usually same day"
- **"we recommend X because A, B, and C"**: This is a classic AI recommendation pattern with a tricolon reason list. Instead, name the brand and explain through a negative story: "We fit a lot of Caroma toilets. We've fitted cheaper ones before and had customers ring back within a year."
- **"[Service] is $X to $Y and takes [time]"**: This clean informational template reads as AI. Break it up: "Toilet repair is $160 to $260. That covers the inlet valve or outlet valve, takes about half an hour."
- **"in line with"**: Formal phrase. Use "pretty much what they quoted" or similar.
- **Clean Q&A with neat wrap-up**: FAQ answers should be messy, not perfectly structured. Don't end with "Ring us and we'll give you a timeframe."

### EMOTIONAL AND REFLECTIVE AI PATTERNS (discovered from 71% → 87% on celebrant)
AI tries to sound human by being emotional and reflective. These patterns are strong AI signals:
- **Escalating emotional lists**: Listing mundane things building to an emotional climax: "backyards, beaches, pub kitchens, and once in a hospital room because..." AI loves this structure. Keep lists flat, don't build to an emotional payoff.
- **Sentimental reflections**: "That one still gets me." / "I'll never forget that." / "It's why I do what I do." Delete these entirely.
- **Empathy conditionals**: "If your budget is tight..." / "If you're worried about..." This is AI trying to be sensitive and understanding. Just state the cheaper option directly: "Elopements are $650."
- **Balanced contrast hedging**: "personal and relaxed but still has weight" / "not too casual but not too formal either." AI tries to sound nuanced by balancing two qualities. Pick one side: "They don't want anything formal."
- **"honestly"**: AI filler word used to simulate authenticity. Maximum 1 per page, and only in a review.
- **High "I" frequency**: In first-person content, AI overuses "I" (3%+ of words). The 99% electrician text had "I" at 0.1%. Keep "I" usage low even in first-person voice by focusing sentences on "you" or the service.
The end of pages consistently scores as AI because it's all clean process/CTA/list content. This is especially damaging in shorter content (under 1000 words) where the ending is a larger percentage of the total text. Fixing this raised the lawn mowing page from 68% to 91%.
- **Suburb lists**: NEVER use a clean comma-separated list of suburbs. Instead break it into conversational sentences: "Most of our work is in Penrith and Kingswood because that's where we started. But we go out to Emu Plains, Glenmore Park and Cranebrook too."
- **Process sections**: NEVER write clean step-by-step instructions. Ramble instead: "Most people just text us their address and we can give you a price from that, we know most of the streets well enough to quote without seeing it."
- **CTAs**: NEVER use polished calls to action like "Ring or text for a quote, 7 days." Make it casual: "Text us your address and we'll get back to you."
- **Reviews**: NEVER follow the template "Positive statement → price → minor complaint → qualifier." Real reviews tell a mini-story, often starting with the problem or a comparison to a previous bad experience: "we tried 2 other lawn mowing guys before these ones, first bloke just stopped showing up"
- **"No lock-in contracts, you can cancel any time"**: This reads as a sales page. Rewrite: "there's no contract or anything like that, if you want to stop you just tell us"

## STRUCTURE
- Keep total word count between 1,500 and 2,000 words
- This is a SERVICE PAGE, not an about-us essay. Structure for the customer and for Google's crawl efficiency:
  1. **H1 with keyword** (e.g. "Mac Repair Sydney")
  2. **Opening paragraph** (2-3 sentences: what you do, where, key differentiator like "free diagnosis" or "same day")
  3. **Services** (each service as its own section with: what it is, what it costs, how long, a customer benefit or outcome. This is the longest part of the page, 60-70% of content)
  4. **Pricing summary** (all prices in one place for quick reference, with context on value vs alternatives)
  5. **How it works / process** (3-4 short paragraphs walking customer through what happens)
  6. **Client reviews** (3-4 reviews with natural errors)
  7. **FAQ** (short answers, 1-2 sentences each, factual)
  8. **Contact details** (simple, no hard sell)
- First 100 words must tell Google and the visitor EXACTLY what this page is about. No warm-up, no throat-clearing.
- Every paragraph must earn its place. If it doesn't help the customer make a decision, cut it.
- Headings should sound like a person would say them: "Mac screen replacement in Sydney" not "Our Comprehensive Screen Repair Services"
- Short paragraphs (3-5 sentences max). This saves Google crawl resources and improves readability.
- Combine short declarative sentences into longer run-on sentences to keep average sentence length at 16-20 words. Short punchy sentences (under 10 words) score as AI when there are too many of them.

## STEP 3: MANDATORY PRE-OUTPUT REVIEW

After drafting the ENTIRE piece, run ALL of these checks before outputting. If any fail, fix them and re-check. DO NOT output until all pass.

1. **WORD COUNT**: 1,500-2,000 words? If not, adjust.
2. **AND/BUT/SO COUNT**: Count every sentence starting with And/But/So/Or. Is it 15%+? If not, change more openers.
3. **KEYWORD DENSITY**: Count the exact primary keyword phrase. Is it 8-15? If over 15, remove instances. If under 8, add naturally.
4. **KEYWORD SPAM CHECK**: Does the primary keyword appear more than once per 100 words on average? If yes, it's spam. Remove instances.
5. **BANNED PHRASE SCAN**: Check for every banned phrase. Any found = rewrite.
6. **EM-DASH SCAN**: Any em-dashes? Replace with parentheses or new sentences.
7. **TRICOLON SCAN**: Any parallel groups of three? Break them.
8. **SYNONYM SCAN**: Any place a synonym was used to avoid repetition? Change back to the repeated word.
9. **WRAP-UP SCAN**: Does any section end with a neat conclusion? Delete it.
10. **REVIEW CHECK**: 3-4 reviews with natural errors present?
11. **PAIN POINT CHECK**: Does the content address: pricing, trust, quality, process, location, and comparison anxiety? If any are missing, add them.
12. **VALUE CHECK**: Would a real searcher find this page useful? Does it answer their actual questions with specific information? If any section is filler, replace with useful content.
13. **HEDGING PATTERN SCAN** (CRITICAL - this check alone raised a score from 68% to 78%):
    - Count "depending on": must be 0-2 maximum. If over 2, rewrite.
    - Count "which is/means/makes/was": must be 0 in body text (OK in reviews). If any found, rewrite.
    - Count parentheses: must be 0-2 maximum. If over 2, rewrite as inline text.
    - Count "usually": must be under 6. If over, remove or replace with direct statements.
    - Count "we recommend/suggest": must be 0. Replace with what you actually do: "We fit a lot of X" not "We recommend X".
    - Scan for "[Service] is $X to $Y and takes [time]" template: if the same structure appears more than twice, vary it.
14. **TEMPLATE PATTERN SCAN**: Read through the service sections. Do multiple paragraphs follow the same structure (name service → state price → state timeframe → explain)? If yes, vary the structure. Start some with the price, some with a problem, some with what happens.

ALL 14 checks must pass before output. This is the difference between 47% and 96%.

## PATTERNS FROM REAL 99% HUMAN WEBSITES (The Electronic Fix, Brisbane)

These patterns were found by analyzing a real small business website that scores 97-99% human on Originality.ai. Many of these CONTRADICT our earlier statistical targets, but they work because they're how real business owners actually write.

### What real business websites do that AI doesn't:

1. **HONEST REFERRALS AND LIMITATIONS**: Real businesses admit what they can't do and refer customers elsewhere. AI never does this because it's programmed to sell. Include lines like "If it's a warranty job Apple will do it for free and we'll tell you that upfront" or "For solar panel work you're better off with a specialist sparky, we can point you to one." Admitting limitations and referring to specialists is a STRONG human and trust signal. It shows you care about the customer getting the right result, not just getting the job.

2. **TECHNICAL JARGON AND MODEL NUMBERS**: Real tradespeople use part codes (A1932, A1708), technical terms (SMD, GPU, ultrasonic, logic board, solder joints, DC jack), and brand-specific terminology. AI writes generically. Include model numbers and part codes wherever relevant.

3. **PRICE TABLES WITH MODEL NUMBERS**: List specific prices for specific models: "A1278 MacBook Pro battery @ $169. A1466 MacBook Air @ $189." This is extremely human. AI writes price ranges; humans write price lists.

4. **PHONE NUMBER IN BODY TEXT**: Weave the phone number into the body text 2-3 times, not just in the footer. "Give us a call (07) 3613 0029 as we can repair most graphics card faults." Real websites do this constantly.

5. **BUSINESS NAME REPEATED**: Use the actual business name in the body text 3-5 times. "At The Electronic Fix we charge $125 per hour." AI tends to use "we" without the business name.

6. **COLLOQUIAL/CASUAL PHRASES**: "just pop into the store", "best running it into us", "it does not cost an Arm & a Leg", "Chances are...", "Don't worry." These are sales-casual phrases real business owners use that AI almost never generates.

7. **ALL CAPS FOR EMPHASIS**: Use ALL CAPS sparingly for emphasis: "BUT FEAR NOT", "Please NOTE:", "Quote You A Fixed price." This is a strong human signal. Use 1-2 times per page maximum.

8. **INCONSISTENT CAPITALISATION**: Real websites have inconsistent caps: "MacBook Pro" then "macbook pro", "Graphics card" then "graphics chip", "LogicBoard" as one word. Don't standardise everything perfectly.

9. **SAME POINT MADE TWICE**: Real business owners repeat their key selling points in different sections. The Electronic Fix explains their "fixed price quote not hourly rate" benefit in THREE different places. This repetition is very human — AI never repeats the same point.

10. **LONG RAMBLING SENTENCES**: The real 99% page has an average sentence length of 27.6 words with sentences over 40 words. These are genuine run-ons where the writer keeps adding clauses: "We repair a lot of devices that others will not attempt to repair just simple things like your Graphics card on your iMac, well this is a simple task for an experienced technician and can be done as a next day repair."

11. **EXCLAMATION MARKS**: Real small business websites use exclamation marks naturally: "I can highly recommend!" "Chances are, any problem you may have is something we have seen and fixed before!" Use 2-4 per page.

12. **"We do" / "We can" / "We also" REPETITION**: 31% of sentences on the real page start with "We". This repetitive pattern is human because it's how business owners naturally write — they keep saying what they do. Don't force variety in sentence starters.

## PATTERNS FROM 99% HUMAN SPOKEN LANGUAGE (Julius Sumner Miller transcript)

A spoken lecture transcript scored 99% human. While you can't write a service page like a lecture, these spoken-language patterns can be adapted for written content to push scores higher:

1. **"AND" CHAINING**: Spoken language chains clauses with "and" endlessly. 5% of all words in the transcript were "and". In service pages, chain ideas with "and" instead of starting new sentences: "we pick your dog up and walk to the nearest park and do the walk and bring them home and make sure they've got water" reads more human than breaking that into 4 clean sentences.

2. **SELF-CORRECTION MID-SENTENCE**: Speakers catch themselves and restart. Adapt this for writing: "we used to charge by the hour but we found that... actually we still do for some jobs but mostly we quote a fixed price now." The "actually" pivot is a strong human signal.

3. **FILLER TRANSITIONS**: "now", "well", "so", "indeed" used as bridges between ideas. "Now the other thing is..." / "Well the short answer is..." / "So if you need that done..." These are thinking-out-loud words that show a real mind.

4. **DIRECT READER ADDRESS**: "have you ever noticed how...", "you'll see when you bring it in", "I hope you can see why..." This pulls the reader into the content. AI writes AT people; humans write TO them.

5. **GENUINE ENTHUSIASM**: Not "We're passionate about plumbing!" (performative, AI). Instead: "this is honestly the bit we love doing, pulling apart a logic board and finding the one burnt component" (specific, genuine). Enthusiasm about the WORK, not about the business.
